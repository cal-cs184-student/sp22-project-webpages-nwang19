In this assignment, we implemented reflective and refractive surfaces as well as different camera depth of field.

Part 1: Mirror and Glass Materials

In this part, we implemented functions that makes material reflective and refractive.

With 0 ray depth, there are no rays interacting with the scenes so there is just a black background with the light source. With 1 depth, the box is lit up and we can see the outline of the of the spheres. However the spheres are still dark because the only light rays are coming from the source (therefore we can see the reflection). With 2 depth, the left sphere now clearly reflects the end of the box, the right sphere is reflecting less because of it’s refractive properties. Both of the sphere’s shadows become lighter. With 3 depth, we can now see the right sphere’s refractive properties. We can also see that the right sphere is being reflected in the left sphere, creating a mirror tunnel. However parts of that reflection is still dark. With 4 depth, the reflection on the left sphere resembles the right sphere more, with no more dark areas. Ray depth 4 and 5 have no big visual difference, same with 100 rays.

Reflection shows up with less bounces because it is a direct change of direction. With refraction, it has the chance to either refract or reflect, only when a large number of rays have hit the material will both effects show up.

Part 4: Depth of Field

In this part we implemented camera depth of field so that it simulates a thin-lens camera model. A pinhole camera is an “ideal” model where all the rays go through one coordinate and everything is in focus. A thin-lens camera model only have one plane of focus that is its focalDistance away. Everywhere else it is blurry.
